Scout Locations

The Problem

SnapSpot is designed to be a photo location recommendation tool. We have observed that there are many existing map location tools that don't provide the right insights and network for scouting photography locations. How might we develop our app, SnapSpot, into a solution for finding locations vetted among photographers?

The solution

We believe the creation of photo location app will result in an improvement in creativity and best practices because it will increase collaboration between photographers.

What I did?

As a member of my group, I conducted 1:1 interviews with them and collected data after some group user research steps. I created a storyboard, sketched the wireframe, and produced digital wireframes, as well as did usability testing to ensure an effective user interface.

The process

Researched was conducted through the use of user interviews How did you develop interview/survey questions (providing a topic map can help)?  What are the key findings you derived from the interview/survey?  A combined 85% of survey respondents and all interviewees either post or showcase their photos in a public way Location frustration was a primary indicator for prohibiting great photos and shoots for 100% of interviewees Interviewees shared an interest in collaboration with other photographers for development of technique and inspiration  What other products solve a similar problem?  500px, Pixel, Locationscout, and Google Earth. How do they compare/contrast?  None of the evaluated competitors could offer the familiarity of popular social media aspects and the community of unique users to help plan and research location shoots from recommended users.

User Research

When combining interview analysis using empathy maps and affinity diagrams, themes were clear we needed to provide for our users.  In addition to the findings we already discussed, additional findings included…. Seeing a showcase of others’ work Insights to how others scout locations Work with a bona fide community of users Leverage a social platform for inspiration

User Flow

First iteration user flows and sketches gave us the visibility to see how our users would interact with the app. Quickly we learned that dead ends and navigation needed to account for a “home base” for our users We needed to pivot and learn from those errors for the next iteration in design. Updates to user flow gave us many more avenues to include in the “happy path” so that users could make the most of finding the features available

Interactive Prototype

The Lo-fi digital prototype gave us a sneak peek into what we needed to layer into design to make it even better as an MVP Instances of ability to scroll on select pages, log out throughout the app at different interfaces and ability to get back to the “home feed” were added

    • Testing

    Usability testing for the app was a hit. 100% of our users were able to complete the tasks of navigating “home” and “navigating back” to find their way around all the features As an extra win, this gave our testers the platform to share other feedback such as validation that there was no 2 factor authentication, the implementation of the back button on most screens and an easy log-in and log-out function

    Wrapping up

    Discovery and original interviews gave us clear indication using Feature Prioritization that a post-MVP iteration could provide possible monetary value for our users. The interviewees gave us lots of insight how the photos could be used to build future clientele, the app could become a marketplace to sell Getty Images or attract gallery attention. Finally another post MVP feature could involve publishing articles for aspiring users, workshop opportunities and more. Exciting features ahead… At this time we would like to wrap the case study
